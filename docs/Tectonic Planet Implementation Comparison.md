# **Architectural Reconciliation and Divergence Analysis: Cortial Tectonic Planet Implementations**

The implementation of procedural planetary bodies based on tectonic geodynamics requires an exacting adherence to the underlying kinematic and geometric algorithms that govern lithospheric deformation over immense simulated timescales. The 2019 paper "Procedural Tectonic Planets" by Yann Cortial, Adrien Peytavie, Eric Galin, and Eric Guérin, alongside Cortial’s 2020 doctoral thesis "Modélisation de terrains planétaires réalistes," establishes a highly specific procedural framework designed to synthesize large-scale geological phenomena without the computationally prohibitive costs of full physically-based continuum mechanics simulations.1 These foundational texts dictate the precise mechanisms for crustal resampling, subduction uplift, continental collision, and the driving forces of plate motion.  
The subsequent analysis provides a systematic comparison between the proposed v5 architecture specification for an Unreal Engine 5 implementation and the documented methodologies of the original source material. The objective is to identify critical deviations, simplifying assumptions, and omitted mechanical details that compromise the structural integrity of the generated spherical mesh or the geological realism of the resulting terrain. Particular attention is devoted to the documented failure states—such as catastrophic stippling, degenerate stretched triangles, and boundary region gaps—and how these visual artifacts are direct mathematical consequences of architectural divergence from the thesis.

## **1\. Resampling and the Reconciliation Pipeline**

The procedural tectonic planet model relies fundamentally on a discrete temporal and spatial resampling pipeline to manage the dynamic evolution of the planetary crust as tectonic plates continuously diverge, converge, and fracture across the spherical domain. The proposed v5 architecture outlines a methodology utilizing an interior-only containment soup coupled with a neighbor-majority plate-switch guard, relying on a self-copy mechanism to bridge boundary-region gaps. This architectural decision represents a fundamental departure from the primary source material, specifically Chapter 4 of Cortial’s 2020 doctoral thesis, which specifies a highly controlled, multi-stage geometric subdivision methodology dependent on ghost vertex duplication.

### **1.1 Source Material Baseline vs. Implementation**

In the 2020 thesis, Cortial details a sophisticated approach to guarantee watertight geometry ("géométrie étanche") at the boundaries of varying levels of detail.3 Rather than relying on heuristic-based plate reassignment for ambiguous boundary triangles, the target level of detail for the triangulation is achieved through an iterative, deterministic sequence of four distinct operations. This sequence is explicitly defined as the division of edges (division d'arêtes), the marking of ghost vertices (marquage des sommets-fantômes), the division of ghost edges (division des arêtes-fantômes), and finally, the division of faces (division des faces).3  
The critical mechanism within this pipeline is the deployment of the ghost vertices. These "sommets fantômes" are deliberately placed at the exact mathematical midpoint of non-divided edges that belong to faces requiring subdivision—specifically, triangles that possess at least one divided edge.3 This four-step treatment encapsulates a single level of subdivision and is repeated iteratively until no edges remain that require division to reach the target geometric resolution.3 Furthermore, the thesis dictates a highly specific sequencing for the resolution of these topological anchors. Following the injection of hydrographic models used for the creation of riverbeds and adjoining valleys, the modeling phase concludes with the repositioning of the ghost vertices affected by the prescription of transverse sections, followed by the recalculation of normals.3  
In direct contrast, the v5 architecture abandons this deterministic subdivision locking mechanism in favor of an interior-only containment algorithm. When boundary triangles fall into ambiguous states between diverging or converging plates, the implementation relies on a neighbor-majority rule to force a plate switch, subsequently utilizing self-copy routines to patch the resulting topological gaps.

### **1.3 Anticipated Consequences of the Deviation**

The failure modes previously encountered during the development process—specifically the persistent twenty percent boundary gap and the catastrophic stippling artifacts at higher plate counts—are direct, unavoidable mathematical consequences of abandoning the ghost vertex methodology. The v5 architecture's reliance on a neighbor-majority heuristic fundamentally assumes that the ratio of unassigned boundary triangles to unambiguously assigned interior triangles remains low enough that the interior mass provides a stable, overwhelming anchor for topological resolution.  
This assumption breaks down entirely as the number of simulated tectonic plates increases. The perimeter-to-area ratio of a tectonic plate scales inversely with the total number of plates on the spherical domain. At twenty plates, as utilized in the referenced Driftworld implementation, the interior surface area of an average plate is sufficiently massive that the majority-rule algorithm functions adequately; the boundary triangles are rapidly and consistently absorbed by the overwhelmingly large parent plate. However, when the simulation scales to forty plates, the average surface area of each plate is halved, but the perimeter length governing the boundary fault lines does not decrease at a proportional rate. Consequently, the percentage of the total mesh residing within the ambiguous "boundary-region gap" increases exponentially.  
By utilizing interior-only containment at this scale, the algorithm inadvertently generates vast topological "no-man's-lands." When the neighbor-majority rule attempts to evaluate these disputed zones, it encounters competing, equally weighted mathematical claims from neighboring boundary triangles rather than referencing stable interior triangles. This parity causes rapid, frame-by-frame oscillation in plate assignment, which manifests visually as the described catastrophic stippling.  
The ghost vertex methodology outlined in the 2020 thesis solves this issue entirely by circumventing algorithmic assignment in favor of geometric constraint. By mathematically forcing a temporary vertex at the exact midpoint of a shared edge and locking the topology before subdivision occurs, the algorithm prevents the creation of gaps from the outset.3 The absence of this mechanical locking mechanism in the v5 architecture is precisely what permits the spherical mesh to tear, creating the twenty percent gap. Furthermore, the previous attempts to utilize per-vertex rotation of boundary triangles resulted in degenerate stretched triangles precisely because those boundary vertices were not anchored to ghost midpoints prior to the rotational transformation being applied. Without the ghost vertices acting as intermediate pivot points, the rotational vectors pulled the raw boundary vertices across diverging geodetic trajectories, instantly stretching the associated triangles beyond their degenerate limits.

### **1.4 Non-Obvious Implementation Details**

A highly subtle but vital architectural detail embedded in the 2020 thesis is the precise temporal sequencing of the ghost vertex resolution. The repositioning of the ghost vertices occurs strictly after the injection of the hydrographic modeling that carves riverbeds and valleys into the terrain.3 This indicates that the ghost vertices temporarily act as rigid, immutable anchors during the terrain amplification and erosion passes, preserving the integrity of the plate boundaries while the interior elevations fluctuate wildly due to simulated water flow. If the v5 architecture attempts to utilize self-copy algorithms to bridge boundary gaps after independent plate elevations and hydrographic erosion have already been calculated, the implementation will inherently force vertical shearing along the fault lines, destroying the visual coherence of the planetary crust and resulting in jagged, mismatched cliffs along every tectonic boundary.

## **2\. Subduction Mechanics and Uplift Modeling**

In the Cortial procedural model, subduction is the primary geodynamic driver of lithospheric deformation. The process occurs at convergent boundaries where the crust of an oceanic plate plunges beneath a second plate, generating deep oceanic trenches, triggering intense volcanism, and causing severe deformation and orogeny within the overriding plate.2 The fidelity of the resulting mountain ranges depends entirely on the rigorous mathematical implementation of the uplift formulas and the forces dictating the plunging slab.

### **2.1 Source Material Baseline vs. Implementation**

According to the 2019 "Procedural Tectonic Planets" paper, the uplift applied to the overriding plate during a subduction event is not a generalized volume displacement, but a precise composition of mathematical functions evaluated continuously across the affected crust. The uplift $u\_{ej}$ of the overriding plate $P\_j$ at a given point $p$ is defined as $u\_{ej}(p) \= u\_0 \\cdot f(d(p)) \\cdot g(v(p)) \\cdot h(ez\_i(p))$.2 Within this equation, $u\_0$ represents the base subduction uplift, $f$ is a distance transfer function, $g$ is a speed transfer function, and $h$ is a height transfer function.2  
The proposed v5 architecture specifies the use of a piecewise cubic falloff for the uplift profile, which aligns with the thesis material. The source material explicitly defines $f(d)$ as a piecewise cubic curve featuring a maximum at a control distance from the boundary, which subsequently fades to zero as the distance reaches the maximum subduction distance.2 However, the v5 architecture dictates that the distance-to-front variable $d(p)$ is computed using a Breadth-First Search (BFS) algorithm traversing a Delaunay graph.  
Furthermore, the physical motion of the subducting plate is governed by the accumulation of slab pull torque. The 2019 paper dictates that the slab pull mechanism updates the rotation axis $\\omega\_i$ of the plunging plate to draw it closer to the subduction front. This is calculated using the specific vector summation formula: $\\omega\_i(t \+ \\delta t) \= \\omega\_i(t) \+ \\epsilon \\sum\_{k=0}^{n-1} \\frac{c\_i \\times q\_k}{\\|c\_i \\times q\_k\\|} \\delta t$, where $c\_i$ represents the geometric center of the plate, $q\_k$ represents distinct points located along the subduction front, and $\\epsilon$ serves as a scaling parameter.2 Finally, the v5 architecture proposes triangle exclusion during containment to isolate subduction zones, a technique not present in the source material.

### **2.3 Anticipated Consequences of the Deviation**

The architectural decision to utilize a Breadth-First Search (BFS) on a Delaunay graph to calculate the distance-to-front variable $d(p)$ introduces a severe topological vulnerability into the core geodynamic simulation. A Delaunay triangulation projected onto a spherical domain—particularly one that is procedurally generated or derived from an icosphere or cube-sphere projection—inherently contains a heterogeneous distribution of edge lengths. A BFS algorithm is strictly topological; it measures "distance" solely in terms of discrete edge hops rather than true geometric distance measured in kilometers or spherical radians.  
Because the central uplift function $f(d(p))$ is highly sensitive to the input distance, feeding a topological edge-count into a piecewise cubic curve will violently warp the resulting orogenic uplift.2 In regions of the planetary mesh where the Delaunay tessellation is particularly dense, the BFS-calculated distance will climb rapidly over a very short physical geographic space, causing the cubic uplift curve to spike prematurely and compress unnaturally. Conversely, in regions where the mesh tessellation is sparse, the BFS distance will accumulate slowly, artificially stretching the uplift profile far into the interior of the overriding plate. Visually, this divergence will manifest as irregular, pulsing, or highly "lumpy" mountain ranges along the subduction front, entirely destroying the smooth, sweeping, continent-spanning cordilleras (such as the Andes) that the Cortial algorithm is designed to produce.3 To maintain fidelity to the source material and ensure visually coherent orogeny, the distance variable $d(p)$ must be evaluated using a true great-circle distance algorithm (such as the Haversine formula) to the interpolated subduction boundary line.  
Similarly, the proposed technique of triangle exclusion during the containment phase introduces significant risk. The original architecture relies on evaluating the uplift function on a per-sample-point basis during the continuous resampling phase, which occurs every ten to sixty time steps.2 By dynamically excluding triangles to isolate subduction zones, the v5 architecture risks creating artificial voids in the crustal density calculations, which can lead to non-manifold artifacts during the subsequent terrain amplification phase.

### **2.4 Non-Obvious Implementation Details**

A critical geodynamic insight provided in the source material lies within the height transfer function $h(ez\_i(p))$. The 2019 paper dictates that this function utilizes the normalized elevation of the plunging plate, $ez\_i(p)$, but specifically squares that value: $h(ez\_i) \= ez\_i^2(p)$.2 The normalization is calculated with respect to the maximum depth of oceanic trenches $z\_t$ and the highest continental elevation $z\_c$, expressed as $ez\_i(p) \= (z\_i(p) \- z\_t) / (z\_c \- z\_t)$.2  
This non-linear, squared scaling is not a cosmetic choice; it is the fundamental mechanical trigger for "forced subductions".2 Because the value is squared, standard, low-elevation oceanic crust plunging into the mantle generates minimal values, causing negligible overriding deformation. However, when high-relief oceanic features, such as old mid-ocean ridges or massive oceanic plateaus, reach the trench, the squared function causes an exponential, violent spike in the calculated uplift.2 This specific mathematical relationship is what allows the simulation to procedurally generate massive, localized orogenic events without manual intervention. If the squaring operation is omitted, forced subductions will fail to materialize, and the planet will lack dramatic continental deformation.

## **3\. Continental Collision and Terrane Accretion**

While subduction governs the interaction between oceanic and continental crust, a fundamental shift in geodynamics occurs when two converging tectonic plates both carry highly buoyant continental crust. Because continental mass is too light to be dragged deeply into the mantle by slab pull, the plates interpenetrate, leading to extreme vertical uplift, the folding of the lithosphere, and the transfer of crustal fragments known as terranes.2

### **3.1 Source Material Baseline vs. Implementation**

The Cortial 2019 model does not treat continental collision as a continuous mathematical curve akin to oceanic subduction, but rather as a highly specific, discrete geological event triggered by plate configuration parameters. When the simulation detects a continental-continental configuration, it immediately initiates a forced subduction protocol that rapidly evolves into a full collision.2 At this exact moment, a "slab break" is triggered.2 This causes a terrane $R$ from the initiating plate $P\_i$ to cease its geodetic migration and permanently suture to the opposing continental plate $P\_j$.2  
To calculate the geographic area affected by this massive collision, the source material utilizes a precise geometric expansion. The uplift region is mathematically defined as the region of the sutured terrane $R$ expanded by a ball of radius $r$, written as the Minkowski addition $R \\oplus B(r)$.2 This operation ensures that the deformation bleeds naturally into the surrounding crust.  
The most profound divergence in the proposed v5 architecture lies in the methodology for calculating crustal folding. The v5 document specifies the use of "collision uplift with centroid folding." However, the 2019 paper strictly defines the lithospheric folding direction $f\_j$ as a temporally accumulated vector field. This vector field is updated iteratively at every single discrete time step $\\delta t$ using the formula: $f\_j(p, t \+ \\delta t) \= f\_j(p, t) \+ \\beta(s\_i(p) \- s\_j(p)) \\delta t$.2 In this equation, $\\beta$ operates as a weighting constant, while $s\_i(p)$ and $s\_j(p)$ represent the instantaneous geodetic speeds of the colliding plates.2

| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Event Trigger Mechanism** | Discrete slab break triggered upon strict continental-continental crust contact.2 | Slab break trigger mechanism. | **Aligned** |
| **Zone of Uplift Influence** | Precise expansion of the sutured terrane via Minkowski addition $R \\oplus B(r)$.2 | Terrane detection and localized area expansion. | **Cosmetic / Aligned** |
| **Crustal Folding Algorithm** | Iterative, temporal accumulation of relative speed vectors over simulated history.2 | Static spatial evaluation via centroid folding. | **Critical** |

### **3.3 Anticipated Consequences of the Deviation**

The implementation of "centroid folding" within the v5 architecture represents a catastrophic misunderstanding of the geological principles underpinning the Cortial procedural model. A centroid-based folding algorithm is inherently static and purely spatial; it calculates fold directions by indiscriminately pointing surface normals toward or away from the geometric center of the colliding plates, or strictly perpendicular to the collision boundary line, at a single, isolated moment in time.  
By contrast, the source formula $f\_j(p, t \+ \\delta t) \= f\_j(p, t) \+ \\beta(s\_i(p) \- s\_j(p)) \\delta t$ represents a continuous temporal integration.2 This formula dictates that the vast geological history of the tectonic plate strictly governs the orientation of the resulting mountain ranges. During the millions of years required for a continental collision to complete, it is highly common within the simulation for plates to slightly alter their trajectory as global forces shift. Because the Cortial formula integrates these vectors over time, the resulting fold vectors exhibit beautifully curved, complex, and heterogeneous orientations that perfectly mimic the chaotic thrusting of real-world geology.  
A static centroid fold algorithm completely overwrites this rich kinematic history, producing highly uniform, artificially straight ridges that instantly betray their procedural nature. This divergence becomes fatal during the subsequent terrain amplification phase. To generate high-resolution relief, the coarse crust model is amplified by placing procedural landform primitives.2 Crucially, these primitives are shifted and rotated within the tangent plane of the sphere to align perfectly with the local fold direction vector $f\_j$.2 If the underlying vector field is generated by a rudimentary centroid algorithm, every single terrain primitive will align parallel to the boundary in robotic, uniform rows. The generated mountain ranges will entirely lack the characteristic natural splaying, branching, and structural complexity observed in massive orogenies such as the Himalayas.

### **3.4 Non-Obvious Implementation Details**

The implementation of terrane transfer requires the algorithm to physically detach crustal samples from plate $P\_i$ and reattach them to the topological structure of plate $P\_j$. The non-obvious requirement embedded in the source material is the strictly scheduled timing of this operation. Terrane suturing and sample transfer must be executed exclusively during the discrete, batched *resampling* phase, which occurs only every ten to sixty time steps.2 Attempting to execute terrane suturing dynamically on every single frame, or during every discrete continuous movement tick $\\delta t$, will inevitably lead to massive computational bottlenecks and immediately induce non-manifold boundary states as the topology attempts to heal itself faster than the mesh data structure allows.

## **4\. Plate Rifting Dynamics**

To prevent the simulation from stagnating into a single, static super-continent, the procedural model requires a mechanism for continuous planetary evolution. Plate rifting serves as this mechanism, operating as a discrete, catastrophic event that fractures a massive parent plate into several smaller, diverging sub-plates.2

### **4.1 Source Material Baseline vs. Implementation**

The Cortial 2019 framework does not treat rifting as a gradual tearing process, but rather as an instantaneous geometric fracture dictated by a probabilistic model. The probability of a rift event occurring is governed by a Poisson Law ($\\lambda$).2 To ensure geological logic, this probability parameter $\\lambda$ is dynamically scaled based on two primary factors: the relative geographic size of the continent in question, and the continental crust type ratio of the parent plate, mathematically denoted as $x\_P$.2  
When the Poisson probability triggers an event, the algorithm distributes a specific number of centroids, $n$ (where $n$ is strictly between two and four), at random positions within the boundaries of the original parent plate.2 The algorithm then constructs Voronoi cells radiating outward from these centroids to define the initial, raw geometric shapes of the new sub-plates.2 However, to prevent the formation of mathematically perfect, straight-edged coastlines, the model mandates that the boundaries of these Voronoi cells—defining the fracture curve ($\\Gamma$)—are heavily warped using a procedural noise function before the plates actually separate.2

### **4.2 Divergence Assessment: Plate Rifting Dynamics**

| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Event Trigger Parameter** | Poisson Law ($\\lambda$) dynamically scaled by size and continental ratio $x\_P$.2 | Poisson-triggered fracture event. | **Aligned** |
| **Base Partitioning Algorithm** | Voronoi cell generation utilizing $n \\in $ random centroids.2 | Standard Voronoi partitioning. | **Aligned** |

### **4.3 Anticipated Consequences of the Deviation**

While the core logic of utilizing Poisson triggers and Voronoi partitioning perfectly matches the 2019 paper, the v5 architectural specification notably omits any mention of boundary warping via procedural noise. Standard Voronoi partitioning inherently yields perfectly straight edges and sharp, mathematically rigid vertices. If the simulation allows the newly fractured tectonic plates to diverge along these unwarped Voronoi boundaries, the resulting planetary coastlines will be visibly polygonal and entirely unnatural.  
In the realm of high-end procedural terrain generation, the presence of long, perfectly straight fault lines drastically reduces the visual realism of the final render. The thesis model relies explicitly on the noise-warping of the fracture curve ($\\Gamma$) to synthesize jagged, complex continental shelves.2 If this mathematical warping step is omitted or simplified in the Unreal Engine 5 implementation, the newly formed oceans that generate between the diverging plates will resemble rigid, artificial canals rather than organic geological formations.

### **4.4 Non-Obvious Implementation Details**

A critical, non-obvious implementation detail occurs in the milliseconds immediately following the fracture event. When a parent plate rifts into two to four child sub-plates, the system must instantly assign new, highly divergent random geodetic directions to each of the resulting fragments.2 If the simulation defaults to allowing the original parent plate's angular velocity to be inherited uniformly by the child plates without injecting a massive, overriding diverging vector at the exact moment of fracture, the child plates will simply continue to travel together across the globe in tight formation. Because the algorithm now legally recognizes the boundary between them as an active oceanic ridge, but there is no actual geographic divergence occurring to open the crust, the simulation will rapidly enter a degenerate state. The ridge generation logic will fire repeatedly, but because no physical space is being created, extreme localized geometry overlap will occur, eventually crashing the mesh generation pipeline.

## **5\. Force Model and Plate Motion**

The macroscopic kinematic evolution of the synthetic planet is entirely dependent on the precise calculation of the forces acting upon each tectonic plate. These forces update the angular velocities of the plates, driving them across the spherical surface. To maintain computational stability and visual coherence, these velocities must be rigorously integrated over time and strictly capped.

### **5.1 Source Material Baseline vs. Implementation**

In the Cortial architecture, the motion of tectonic plates across the spherical surface is governed by geodetic speeds $s(p)$ and a central rotational axis $\\omega$.2 The physical forces driving this movement are applied mathematically as torques acting directly upon the plate's center of mass. The two primary geodynamic forces in the simulation are the "pull" exerted by subducting oceanic slabs dragging the plate downward, and the "push" exerted by the generation of new crust at divergent mid-ocean ridges.5  
A critical detail establishing the realism of the Cortial model is found in the 2020 thesis, which explicitly identifies the relative magnitude of these forces. The thesis states unequivocally that the mechanism of Slab Pull ("la force d'entraînement et de traction exercée par la portion plongeante") is the primary engine of subduction and is more important by an entire order of magnitude than the pushing force generated along oceanic ridges ("plus important d'un ordre de grandeur que la poussée le long des dorsales océaniques").3  
To simulate this movement, the model utilizes a discrete temporal integration strategy. The simulation relies on a discrete time-step ($\\delta t$) that is typically set to represent exactly 2 million years (2My) of geological time per tick.2 Because physical torques are being integrated iteratively, the algorithm requires strict velocity capping to prevent runaway kinematic acceleration, particularly when a plate’s mass becomes extremely small or when multiple global forces temporarily align in perfect unison.

| **Force Torque Weighting** | Slab pull is explicitly weighted an order of magnitude stronger than ridge push.3 | General torque accumulation calculation. | **Critical** (If uniform weighting is applied) |

### **5.3 Anticipated Consequences of the Deviation**

The v5 architecture document specifies the use of "torque accumulation" but fails to specify the relative weighting of the constituent geodynamic forces. If the implementation assigns equal coefficients to both ridge push and slab pull, the resulting simulation will deviate drastically from the intended Cortial model, failing to replicate basic terrestrial geodynamics. Because real-world oceanic plates (such as the Pacific Plate) are driven primarily by the immense gravitational weight of their own subducting edges pulling them into the mantle, weighting the forces equally will strip the simulated plates of their necessary kinematic aggression. Oceanic plates will fail to accelerate rapidly toward trenches, leading to a sluggish simulation where the massive "forced subductions" required to trigger extreme continental orogeny simply do not occur frequently enough to build interesting planetary topology.  
Furthermore, the implementation of velocity capping must be inextricably linked to the mesh resampling pipeline. The 2019 paper dictates that the resampling and meshing process is executed dynamically every 10 to 60 time steps, with the exact frequency determined strictly by the maximum observed speed of the plates during that interval.2 If the Unreal Engine 5 architecture caps the velocity at a static, arbitrary numerical value to prevent physics instability, but fails to dynamically tie the resampling frequency to that capped velocity, a critical failure will occur at divergent boundaries. Plates moving at or near the cap speed will traverse too much planetary surface area between discrete resampling ticks. The crust generation algorithm will fail to keep pace with the divergence, leading to massive crustal stretching and the failure to generate sufficient oceanic sample points, ultimately resulting in gaping "bald spots" across the simulated ocean floor.

### **5.4 Non-Obvious Implementation Details**

In the complex vector equation used for updating the rotation axis via slab pull—$\\omega\_i(t \+ \\delta t) \= \\omega\_i(t) \+ \\epsilon \\sum\_{k=0}^{n-1} \\frac{c\_i \\times q\_k}{\\|c\_i \\times q\_k\\|} \\delta t$ 2—the normalization of the cross product (represented by dividing by the magnitude $\\|c\_i \\times q\_k\\|$) is of paramount importance. This mathematical normalization ensures that the sheer geographic distance of the subduction front from the plate's center of mass does not disproportionately skew the magnitude of the resulting torque vector. By normalizing the vector, the torque alters the *direction* of the rotational axis to smoothly steer the plate toward the trench, acting primarily as a kinematic steering mechanism rather than a purely explosive accelerator. Failing to normalize this cross product will result in massive plates experiencing uncontrollable rotational snapping as distant trenches exert mathematically infinite leverage.

## **6\. Oceanic Crust Generation**

As tectonic plates are driven apart by the forces detailed in the previous section, the resulting void must be filled. The procedural model handles this by continuously generating new oceanic crust at divergent boundaries, synthesizing mid-ocean ridges, and establishing the age parameters of the crust, which will subsequently dictate the hierarchy of subduction when plates eventually converge.2

### **6.1 Source Material Baseline vs. Implementation**

The Cortial framework manages oceanic crust generation as a continuous process within the geodynamic simulation logic, but executes it as a discrete operation within the mesh representation pipeline. During the resampling process, new sample points are generated exclusively within the region of the actively diverging ocean ridge.2 Once generated, these new points are immediately assigned to the nearest tectonic plate. Crucially, their foundational crust parameters are computed dynamically based on two distinct spatial metrics: their exact distance from the ridge axis itself, mathematically denoted as $d\\Gamma(p)$, and their distance from the closest overarching plate, denoted as $dP(p)$.2  
As these new crustal points are generated, they are assigned an age of zero. Over simulated time, this age parameter increases. The age of the oceanic crust serves as a direct proxy for its density; as the crust ages, it cools and becomes denser. This parameter is absolutely critical to the convergence logic, as the algorithm dictates that when two oceanic plates converge, the older, denser plate will always subduct beneath the younger, more buoyant plate.2  
Furthermore, the simulation utilizes these distance parameters ($d\\Gamma(p)$) to physically elevate the newly generated crust, forming the mid-ocean ridges. As the crust ages and is pushed further away from the active ridge axis by continuous divergence, it simulates thermal subsidence, gradually losing elevation and sinking into deeper abyssal plains.

| **Crust Point Generation** | Executed strictly during the dynamic resampling phase every 10-60 $\\delta t$ steps.2 | Dynamic point generation assumed. | **Aligned** |
| **Ridge Elevation Mechanics** | Elevation is physically encoded into point parameters via distance functions.2 | Ridge elevation blending. | **Critical** |
| **Age Evaluation and Density** | Age parameter dictates crustal density and strict subduction hierarchy.2 | Age assignment at divergent boundaries. | **Aligned** |

### **6.3 Anticipated Consequences of the Deviation**

While the v5 architecture document does not explicitly state a deviation regarding the raw generation of points, the specific phrasing of "ridge elevation blending" implies a highly dangerous architectural assumption. In game engine development, "blending" typically implies a purely visual, shader-driven, or height-map level post-process operation used to smooth transitions between biomes or terrain types. However, in the Cortial geodynamic model, the elevation of the crust at the mid-ocean ridge is not merely a visual artifact; it is inextricably linked to the underlying physical data structure generated during the resampling step.2  
If the Unreal Engine 5 architecture treats oceanic ridge elevation strictly as a visual blending operation rather than mathematically writing the hard elevation and age data directly into the crust parameters of the newly generated mesh points, the subsequent subduction logic will cascade into failure. As established in Section 2, the subduction algorithm relies entirely on evaluating the physical elevation of the plunging plate via the squared height transfer function $h(ez\_i(p))$ to compute the resulting overriding uplift.2 If the ridge elevations are not baked into the physical points, a scenario where a young, highly elevated oceanic ridge is forced under a continental plate will not register mathematically as a high-relief collision. The simulation will completely fail to register the event, eliminating the extreme, forced-subduction orogeny expected from such a convergence.2 The planetary terrain will remain flat and featureless where massive cordilleras should exist.

### **6.4 Non-Obvious Implementation Details**

A frequently overlooked consequence of the Cortial point generation system is the necessity of aggressive memory management. The resolution of the simulation is highly variable, scaling dynamically from 102 kilometers down to 35 kilometers, yielding a spherical point cloud containing between 60,000 and 500,000 discrete sample points.2 Because oceanic crust is constantly being synthesized at divergent ridges to fill expanding voids, the total point count of the simulation will inflate infinitely over time.  
Therefore, the system must deploy an equally aggressive point culling mechanism at the subduction zones. As crustal points are dragged down into the oceanic trenches and pass beyond the $d(p)$ maximum subduction distance 2, they must be mathematically destroyed and purged from memory. If the age assignment and point generation algorithms located at the ridges do not have a perfectly matched, highly optimized counterpart that deletes points at the trenches, the Unreal Engine 5 implementation will suffer a continuous, fatal memory leak as the underlying data structure expands beyond engine limits over prolonged simulation runtimes.
